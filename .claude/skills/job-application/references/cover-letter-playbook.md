# Cover letter playbook

Synthesised from current (2026) hiring-manager and recruiter guidance, calibrated to small EU JS/TS startups where the cover letter is read by a founder or hiring engineer rather than filtered by HR.

## Length and shape

- **Word count:** 280-360 words. Under 250 reads thin. Over 400 reads indulgent.
- **Paragraphs:** 4. Opening hook, company-specific paragraph, proof paragraph, close.
- **Reading time budget:** the reader spends roughly 7 seconds on the first scan and decides whether to read the rest. Earn the second scan with the first 2 sentences.

## What hiring managers reward

1. **A specific, quantified outcome in the opening.** Not a job title statement, not "I am excited to apply". A concrete result that maps to one of the role's stated needs.
2. **Evidence the candidate researched the company.** Reference a product, a recent launch, a blog post, a customer they have, an engineering choice, or a value the team has stated publicly. Generic praise of "your mission" does not count.
3. **Context the resume cannot give.** Decisions, trade-offs, why a particular technical call was made, what the candidate learned. The resume already tells them _what_; the cover letter tells them _how_ and _why_.
4. **Tone fit for the company.** Bootstrapped product teams reward dry, direct, builder-voice writing. VC-backed startups reward velocity and ownership signals. Larger scaleups reward process maturity and mentorship signals.
5. **A direct close.** "Happy to walk through any of this in more detail" beats "I hope to hear from you" or "I look forward to your response".

## What gets you rejected

Statistics from current hiring-manager surveys:

- 76% auto-reject on typos or grammar errors
- 63% say a bad cover letter disqualifies an otherwise qualified candidate
- 48% auto-reject if the letter is not customised
- The single most-cited mistake is repeating the resume

Specific anti-patterns:

- "I am excited to apply for" / "I am writing to apply for"
- "I am a passionate developer who loves to dive into problems"
- "I am a great team player and excellent communicator"
- Negative framing of the current job
- A list of skills already on the resume
- Cliche AI phrasing: dive into, leverage, seamless, robust, crafting, tapestry, journey, unlock, empower, elevate, streamline, cutting-edge, game-changer, in today's fast-paced world, navigate the complexities, it is worth noting
- Em dashes anywhere

## Structure (use as a template, not a fill-in-the-blanks)

### Paragraph 1: hook (2-4 sentences, ~50-75 words)

Lead with a result from Martin's actual work that maps to a top-stated need in the JD. Name the role and where you saw it in the same paragraph but do not waste sentences on it.

Examples of openings to model:

- "At Trovr I have spent the last year as the sole frontend engineer on two React Native apps and a Next.js admin dashboard, taking the team from no testing strategy and no CI to biweekly App Store and Play Store releases on a Jest + GitLab CI setup I built end to end."
- "Bopkit, a multi-tenant SaaS I designed and built solo, runs subdomain-based storefronts with multi-party PayPal payouts and durable Inngest publishing workflows. The role at <Company> reads like the same problem space at a larger scale."

### Paragraph 2: why this company specifically (2-3 sentences, ~50-75 words)

Prove research. Reference one concrete thing: a product feature, a customer, a hiring post or blog post, an engineering decision visible from the outside, a team member who wrote something publicly, a competitor they are differentiating against. Tie that thing to why Martin is interested.

Avoid: "I love what you are doing", "your mission resonates with me", "I have been a fan for years".

### Paragraph 3: proof, with context (4-6 sentences, ~120-160 words)

One or two stories that demonstrate fit, and that _add detail the resume omits_. Pick the story that maps most directly to the JD's hardest need. Include the decision Martin made and why, not just the outcome.

Examples of context the resume cannot include:

- Why he chose Inngest over a queue-and-worker setup for Bopkit's publishing pipeline
- Why he introduced the testing strategy at Trovr and what tradeoffs he accepted
- How he scoped the multi-tenant subdomain architecture to avoid a per-tenant database
- What he learned shipping a product solo end-to-end vs working in a team

### Paragraph 4: close (1-2 sentences, ~30-40 words)

A direct ask for a conversation. Mention timezone fit if the JD treats it as a constraint. No "I hope to hear from you", no "thank you for your consideration" alone.

Examples:

- "Happy to walk through the Bopkit architecture or the Trovr testing rollout in more detail. CET-based, available to talk this week or next."
- "Would love a 30-minute conversation about how the role is currently scoped. I am in Malta, on CET, and overlap fully with European working hours."

## Adaptation by company type

### Bootstrapped startup (e.g. Featurebase shape)

- Voice: dry, direct, builder. Avoid corporate polish.
- Lead with: capital efficiency, generalist breadth, customer orientation, things shipped solo.
- Reference: founder's writing, the public product, customer testimonials, the team's hiring philosophy if stated.

### VC-backed startup (e.g. Event AI startup shape)

- Voice: ownership and velocity. Some scale-ambition language is fine.
- Lead with: end-to-end ownership, ambiguity tolerance, prior shipping evidence under time pressure.
- Reference: recent launch, funding announcement framing, the technical bet they are making.

### Scaling startup (50+, e.g. Better Stack shape)

- Voice: still direct but more emphasis on team contribution and mentorship.
- Lead with: scope of ownership, technical decisions that affected a team, partnership with adjacent functions.
- Reference: engineering blog, public product evolution, hiring growth.
- If the stack mismatches: address it head-on in the proof paragraph. "I have not shipped Rails in production, but here is a comparable shift I made and how I came up to speed."

### When stack does not match

Open the proof paragraph with the closest analogue Martin has, then explicitly name the gap and how he would close it. Do not pretend the gap does not exist; the reader will spot it. A candid acknowledgement plus evidence of fast ramp on a previous gap usually outperforms hand-waving.

## Voice rules (Martin-specific)

- Never use em dashes. Use colons, periods, parentheses, or commas.
- Never use these words/phrases anywhere: dive into, delve, leverage, seamless, robust, crafting, tapestry, "in today's ... world", "it is worth noting", "navigate the complexities", journey, unlock, empower, elevate, streamline, cutting-edge, game-changer.
- Use first person, past tense for accomplishments. Active verbs. Specific nouns over general ones.
- One claim per sentence. No stacked clauses joined by semicolons.
- British or American spelling: match the company's own writing if you can detect it; otherwise British (Martin's default).

## Final pass checklist

Before handing the file to Martin, run through this:

- [ ] Word count is 280-360
- [ ] Opening sentence contains a specific outcome, not a role statement
- [ ] Paragraph 2 references a concrete, verifiable thing about the company
- [ ] Paragraph 3 includes at least one decision or trade-off the resume does not mention
- [ ] No em dashes anywhere
- [ ] No words from the AI-cliche blocklist
- [ ] No sentence that paraphrases a resume bullet
- [ ] Close has a direct ask, no "I hope to hear from you"
- [ ] Salutation uses a real name if findable, otherwise "Hi <team> team," — never "To whom it may concern"
- [ ] No typos. Read it out loud once.
